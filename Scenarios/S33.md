we master the metrics: **Fact Tables**.

A Fact Table holds the quantitative data of your business—the *how much* and *how many*. But not all events are created equal. A customer buying a coffee is a single, instantaneous event. But an Uber ride is a complex, evolving lifecycle that takes 30 minutes to complete.

If you store an Uber ride the exact same way you store a coffee purchase, your analytical queries will become multi-page SQL nightmares.

Now, we learn the three fundamental **Fact Table Patterns**.

---

### **1. EASY: The "Cash Register" (Transactional Fact Tables)**

**Scenario:** You are building the analytics database for a global coffee chain.
**The Problem:** The business wants to know exactly how many lattes were sold in Seattle between 8:00 AM and 9:00 AM.
**Task:** Design the most granular, foundational fact table possible.

#### **Solution: The Transactional Fact Table**

This is the most common fact table. It represents a single, point-in-time event.

**The Architecture:**

* **The Rule:** One row per event (or one row per line item on a receipt).
* **The Logic:** It is strictly append-only. Once a coffee is sold, that fact never changes.

**The Workflow:**
When a customer buys a coffee and a muffin:

* Row 1: `date_key: 20260308 | store_key: 45 | product_key: 101 (Coffee) | quantity: 1 | revenue: 4.50`
* Row 2: `date_key: 20260308 | store_key: 45 | product_key: 205 (Muffin) | quantity: 1 | revenue: 3.00`

**Production Grade Architecture:**
Because these tables record every single action, they grow massive (billions of rows). They are highly additive. If the CEO asks for "Total Global Revenue," the database simply runs a massive, highly efficient `SUM(revenue)` across the entire table. However, it is terrible for answering questions about *state* (e.g., "What was our inventory level on Tuesday?").

### **2. MEDIUM: The "Bank Balance" (Periodic Snapshot Fact Tables)**

**Scenario:** You are building the data warehouse for a retail bank.
**The Problem:** The CFO wants a dashboard showing the total cash balance of all 10 million checking accounts at the end of every day.
**The Disaster:** If you only have a Transactional Fact Table, calculating the balance for Account A on a random Tuesday requires the database to `SUM` every single deposit and withdrawal since the account was opened 15 years ago. Doing this for 10 million accounts simultaneously will crash the data warehouse.
**Task:** Design a fact table optimized for tracking continuous state over time.

#### **Solution: The Periodic Snapshot Fact Table**

Instead of recording events, we record the *status* of an entity at predefined, regular intervals (daily, weekly, monthly).

**The Architecture:**

* **The Rule:** One row per entity, per time period.
* **The Logic:** Even if no transactions occurred that day, a row is still generated.

**The Workflow:**
At 11:59 PM every night, an Airflow DAG triggers a Spark job that takes a "picture" of the system:

* March 7: `date_key: 20260307 | account_key: 99 | end_of_day_balance: 5000.00 | transactions_today: 0`
* March 8: `date_key: 20260308 | account_key: 99 | end_of_day_balance: 4800.00 | transactions_today: 2`

**Production Grade Architecture:**
Periodic Snapshots are dense. If you have 10 million accounts, you are guaranteed to insert exactly 10 million rows every single night, forever.

* **The "Semi-Additive" Trap:** You cannot sum the metrics across time. If you `SUM(end_of_day_balance)` for March 7 and March 8, the database will say the user has $9,800. That is mathematically false. You can only average balances over time, or sum them across different accounts for the *same* day.

### **3. HARD: The "Lifecycle" (Accumulating Snapshot Fact Tables)**

**Scenario:** You are the Lead Data Engineer at an e-commerce giant (like Amazon or Shopify).
**The Problem:** The Operations team needs to optimize the supply chain. They want to know: *"What is the average time between an order being placed, packed, shipped, and delivered?"*
**The Disaster:** If you use a Transactional Fact Table, one order generates 4 separate rows (Placed, Packed, Shipped, Delivered). To calculate the time differences, the BI tool has to execute a brutal self-join or `PIVOT` operation across 4 rows for millions of orders. The dashboard will time out.
**Task:** Design a fact table optimized for analyzing workflows with clear start and end milestones.

#### **Solution: The Accumulating Snapshot Fact Table**

We abandon the "append-only" rule. We create a single row for the entire lifecycle of an entity, and we *update* that row as it moves through its milestones.

**The Architecture:**

* **The Rule:** One row per workflow instance (e.g., one row per Order ID).
* **The Logic:** The table has multiple date keys representing the standard milestones of the process.

**The Workflow:**

1. **Monday (Order Placed):** We insert the row.
`order_id: 55 | placed_date_key: 20260302 | packed_date_key: NULL | shipped_date_key: NULL`
2. **Tuesday (Order Packed):** We *update* the existing row.
`order_id: 55 | placed_date_key: 20260302 | packed_date_key: 20260303 | shipped_date_key: NULL`
3. **Wednesday (Order Shipped):** We *update* the existing row again.
`order_id: 55 | placed_date_key: 20260302 | packed_date_key: 20260303 | shipped_date_key: 20260304`

**Production Grade Architecture:**
This table structure allows us to pre-calculate the "lag" between milestones.

* We add helper columns to the row: `days_to_pack = 1`, `days_to_ship = 1`.
* When the Operations team opens their dashboard, the average shipping delay is calculated in milliseconds because the complex time-math was already done during the ETL pipeline's `UPDATE` phase.
